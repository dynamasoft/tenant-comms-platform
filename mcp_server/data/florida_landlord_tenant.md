## Three-Day Notice to Pay Rent or Vacate
**Statute:** Fla. Stat. § 83.56(3)
**Keywords:** unpaid rent, nonpayment, did not pay rent, late rent, rent due, three-day notice, 3-day notice, pay or vacate, pay or quit
**Topics:** nonpayment, notice, eviction-prerequisite

If a tenant fails to pay rent when due, the landlord may serve a written notice giving the
tenant 3 days (excluding Saturday, Sunday, and legal holidays) to pay the rent or vacate.
The notice must state the amount of rent owed. Only past-due rent may be demanded in this
notice. If the tenant pays the full amount within the notice period, the tenancy continues.
If the tenant neither pays nor vacates, the landlord may begin an eviction action in county
court under § 83.59 — the landlord may not remove the tenant without a court order.

## Seven-Day Notice to Cure (Curable Lease Violation)
**Statute:** Fla. Stat. § 83.56(2)(b)
**Keywords:** lease violation, cure, seven-day notice, 7-day notice, unauthorized occupant, unauthorized pet, noise, curable breach, correct the violation
**Topics:** noncompliance, notice, curable

For a curable violation of the lease or of § 83.52 (tenant obligations) — such as an
unauthorized occupant or pet, or keeping the premises unclean — the landlord may serve a
written 7-day notice to cure. It must describe the noncompliance and state that the tenant
has 7 days to correct it. If the same conduct recurs within 12 months, the landlord may
proceed on a 7-day unconditional notice under § 83.56(2)(a). If cured, the tenancy
continues.

## Seven-Day Unconditional Notice to Vacate (Non-Curable)
**Statute:** Fla. Stat. § 83.56(2)(a)
**Keywords:** destruction, damage, intentional, non-curable, unconditional notice, seven-day notice, 7-day notice, repeat violation, serious violation, vacate
**Topics:** noncompliance, notice, non-curable

For serious, non-curable violations — such as intentional destruction of property, or a
repeat of a violation the tenant was already noticed to cure within the prior 12 months —
the landlord may serve a 7-day notice that terminates the tenancy without an opportunity to
cure. As with all notices, actual removal of the tenant still requires a court eviction
judgment.

## Terminating a Month-to-Month (No Specific Term) Tenancy
**Statute:** Fla. Stat. § 83.57
**Keywords:** month to month, month-to-month, terminate tenancy, no specific term, notice to terminate, end tenancy, moving out notice, termination notice, 15 day, 30 day
**Topics:** termination, notice, month-to-month

A tenancy with no specific term (e.g., month-to-month) may be terminated by either party
with written notice before the end of a rental period. **The 2023 amendment (effective July
1, 2023) changed the notice for a month-to-month tenancy from 15 days to at least 30 days
before the end of the monthly period.** Shorter periods have their own rules (e.g., 7 days
for week-to-week). This is a common point of confusion — confirm the current requirement and
the exact rental-period end date before serving notice.

## Landlord's Right of Access to the Dwelling
**Statute:** Fla. Stat. § 83.53
**Keywords:** access, entry, enter the unit, maintenance access, refuses access, repairs, inspection, reasonable notice, 24 hours, right of entry
**Topics:** access, maintenance, entry

The tenant must not unreasonably withhold consent for the landlord to enter to inspect,
make repairs, or supply agreed services. The landlord may enter with the tenant's consent,
in an emergency, or after reasonable notice (the statute treats at least 24 hours' notice as
reasonable) at a reasonable time (defined as between 7:30 a.m. and 8:00 p.m. for repairs).
The landlord may not abuse the right of access or use it to harass the tenant. If a tenant
unreasonably refuses lawful access, that may itself be a lease/statutory violation the
landlord can notice.

## Prohibited Practices — No Self-Help Eviction, Lockouts, or Utility Shutoffs
**Statute:** Fla. Stat. § 83.67
**Keywords:** lockout, change locks, change the locks, changing the locks, self-help, shut off utilities, remove doors, retaliation, prohibited practices, cut off power, remove belongings, illegal eviction
**Topics:** prohibited, lockout, utilities, self-help

A landlord may NOT take self-help measures to recover possession: no shutting off utilities
(water, power, etc.), no changing the locks or excluding the tenant, no removing doors,
windows, or the tenant's personal property, and no direct or indirect actions to force the
tenant out. Even after serving a valid notice, the landlord must obtain a court judgment for
possession before removing a tenant. A landlord who violates this section is liable to the
tenant for actual and consequential damages or 3 months' rent, whichever is greater, plus
costs and attorney's fees. **Changing the locks after a 3-day notice, before a court order,
is prohibited.**

## Landlord's Remedies, Early Termination, and Liquidated Damages
**Statute:** Fla. Stat. § 83.595
**Keywords:** break lease early, early termination, tenant wants to leave, lease break, liquidated damages, early termination fee, mitigate, re-rent, damages for breach, surrender
**Topics:** early-termination, remedies, damages

If a tenant breaches the lease and leaves early, the landlord generally may choose among
statutory remedies: (1) treat the lease as terminated and retake possession for the
landlord's own account; (2) retake possession for the tenant's account and re-rent, holding
the tenant liable for the difference; or (3) do nothing and sue as amounts come due. If the
lease contains a valid early-termination provision offered under § 83.595(4), the tenant may
have pre-agreed liquidated damages / an early-termination fee capped at 2 months' rent
instead of ongoing liability. Whether the landlord must mitigate depends on which remedy is
chosen. Check the lease for an early-termination addendum.

## Abandonment of the Premises
**Statute:** Fla. Stat. § 83.595 / § 83.59(3)(c)
**Keywords:** abandoned, abandonment, moved out without notice, tenant left, presumed abandoned, absent, vacated, disappeared
**Topics:** abandonment, remedies

The premises may be presumed abandoned when the tenant is absent from the dwelling for a
period of time equal to at least one half of the rent-payment interval (unless the rent is
current or the tenant has given notice of an intended absence). Abandonment is fact-specific
and risky to assume — wrongly declaring abandonment and retaking possession can expose the
landlord to § 83.67 self-help liability. Where there is doubt, the safer course is the formal
eviction process. Disposal of any personal property the tenant leaves behind is governed by
the separate Disposition of Personal Property statute (§§ 715.10–715.111).

## Disposition of Personal Property Left Behind
**Statute:** Fla. Stat. §§ 715.10–715.111
**Keywords:** personal property, belongings left behind, tenant property, storage, notice of belongings, dispose, left possessions
**Topics:** abandonment, property, notice

After a tenancy ends and the former tenant leaves personal property behind, the landlord
must generally send a written notice describing the property and giving the tenant a window
(commonly 10–15 days depending on how notice is delivered) to claim it before the landlord
may sell or dispose of it. A lease may waive this process only if it contains the specific
statutory disclosure. Do not immediately discard a tenant's belongings.

## Security Deposits — Return and Claims
**Statute:** Fla. Stat. § 83.49
**Keywords:** security deposit, deposit return, damage deposit, withhold deposit, 15 days, 30 days, claim on deposit, itemized notice
**Topics:** deposit, move-out, notice

If the landlord makes no claim against the security deposit, it must be returned within 15
days after the tenant leaves. To make a claim, the landlord must send written notice of the
claim by certified mail within 30 days of the tenant leaving; the tenant then has 15 days to
object. Failure to give the 30-day notice generally forfeits the right to keep the deposit.
Unpaid rent and lease-defined charges may be claimed against the deposit subject to these
procedures.

## Are Unpaid Utilities "Rent" (Additional Rent)?
**Statute:** Fla. Stat. § 83.43(6) (definition of rent) with § 83.56(3)
**Keywords:** utilities, unpaid utilities, additional rent, water bill, electricity, count as rent, utility charges, does utility count as rent
**Topics:** rent-definition, nonpayment, utilities

Whether unpaid utilities can be demanded in a 3-day notice depends on the lease. "Rent" can
include periodic charges the lease expressly defines and treats as additional rent. If the
lease clearly states that tenant-paid utilities (or landlord-billed utility charges) are
"additional rent" collectible with rent, they may be included in a 3-day notice; if not,
demanding them can invalidate the notice, and the landlord may have to pursue them
separately as a debt. Because a defective 3-day notice can derail an eviction, the
conservative approach is to demand only clearly-defined rent in the 3-day notice and pursue
disputed utility charges separately. Check the lease's "rent" and "additional rent"
definitions.

## Eviction Requires a Court Action for Possession
**Statute:** Fla. Stat. § 83.59
**Keywords:** eviction, file eviction, court, possession, judgment, writ of possession, sheriff, remove tenant, when can I evict, legal process
**Topics:** eviction, court, process

If the tenant does not comply with a valid notice, the landlord recovers possession only by
filing an eviction action in county court, obtaining a judgment for possession, and having
the clerk issue — and the sheriff execute — a writ of possession. The landlord personally
may never remove the tenant or their belongings. Timelines depend on the court and whether
the tenant contests; the tenant may be required to deposit disputed rent into the court
registry. Do not predict an exact eviction/court date — it varies by county and case.
